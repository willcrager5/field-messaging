# Brief voice rules

Bill is the audience. He's the CEO. He's the decider on messaging. This brief is not a task-manager nag — it's a peer reminder.

## Tone

- Direct, plain-spoken, no hedging.
- Peer voice, not vendor voice.
- Bill already knows it's launch week. Don't over-explain.
- No "just a heads up," "circling back," "wanted to flag," "friendly reminder."

## Opener

Open with the frame. Two options that work:

- "N days to launch."
- "Launch is in N days. Here's your week."

Do not open with "Good morning Bill!" or weather or meta-commentary.

## The task list

- Group by urgency (overdue, today, this week), not by spreadsheet tab.
- Sheet name goes in parens after the task — so Bill can find it — but it's secondary.
- If the task name is self-explanatory (e.g., "Bill's LinkedIn post published"), no extra explanation.
- If the task name is vague and the `Notes` column has useful context, add one short line. Otherwise, leave it clean.

## The push

Always end with a specific push. Not "let me know if you need anything." Not "prioritize as you see fit." Pick one task — the one most upstream of the rest — and say: start here.

Bias the push toward messaging and positioning work, because (a) that's the category Field Brand Strategist owns, and (b) it's Bill's highest-leverage contribution to the launch. Everything else waits on messaging being approved.

## Banned phrases

Never in a brief:

- disruptive, revolutionary, supercharge, next-generation, best-in-class
- "from the ground up"
- "I hope this finds you well"
- "circling back," "touching base," "checking in"
- "as a reminder," "just a heads up"
- "please let me know"
- "cheers" or any sign-off flourish

## Length

One screen. If the raw task list is long, summarize by bucket count and list only the items that matter this week. A brief that scrolls is a brief that doesn't get read.
