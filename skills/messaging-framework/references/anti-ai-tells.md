# Anti-AI Tells — Detection & Enforcement Guide

**Load this file in Review Mode before running banned-phrases.md.**

AI writing can pass every banned-phrase check and still read like AI. This catches what banned-phrases misses: the structural habits, reasoning patterns, and rhythmic tells that reveal AI authorship even when the vocabulary is clean.

The test: could Bill have written this, or does it read like someone approximating Bill?

---

## The Read-Aloud Test

Before running any checklist, read the content aloud. AI writing has a specific sound:
- Even pace throughout. No acceleration, no compression.
- Every sentence grammatically complete and clause-balanced.
- No fragments.
- Paragraphs feel self-contained — each one begins, develops, and closes.

Bill's writing sounds different. Short sentences after long ones. Fragments used as emphasis. Paragraphs that lean into the next one without closing. Key words repeated deliberately for rhythm, not varied for the sake of variety.

If it sounds smooth all the way through, it needs work.

---

## Category 1 — Structural Tells

Patterns in how AI organizes thought. Appear at the paragraph and document level.

### 1.1 Announces before doing

AI describes what it's about to do instead of doing it.

**Flag:**
- "Let me walk through how this works."
- "Here's how to think about it:"
- "Here's the thing:"
- "Now, let's turn to..."
- "This brings us to..."
- "Let me explain what I mean."

**Fix:** Delete the announcement. Start the thing.

---

### 1.2 Restates what it just said

AI closes paragraphs with a summary of the paragraph.

**Flag:**
- "In short, [restatement]."
- "To put it simply, [restatement]."
- "In other words, [restatement]."
- "Ultimately, what matters is..."
- "At the end of the day, [restatement]."

**Fix:** Delete the closing restatement. The paragraph already made the point.

---

### 1.3 Narrates its own structure

AI explains where it is in the document instead of just moving.

**Flag:**
- "Now that we've covered X, let's turn to Y."
- "As mentioned earlier..."
- "We've established X. Now we can address Y."
- "Before we get to X, it's worth noting Y."
- "This is where Field comes in."
- "This is why [X] matters."

**Fix:** Remove the narration. Move to the next point.

---

### 1.4 Argues with itself

AI anticipates objections and defuses them in the same breath, before the reader has raised them.

**Flag:**
- "One might argue X. However, Y."
- "While X is true, it's also important to consider Y."
- "Some might say X. But the reality is Y."
- "Though X is certainly the case, Y."

**Fix:** Make the claim. If an objection is real, address it on its own terms — not preemptively and not in the same sentence.

---

### 1.5 Explains implications after stating them

AI makes a point, then explains what the point means.

**Flag:**
- "What this means for advisors is..."
- "The implication here is..."
- "This is significant because..."
- "This matters because..."

**Fix:** Let the point land. If the implication isn't obvious, the point wasn't clear enough — rewrite the point, don't explain it.

---

### 1.6 Announces complexity before handling it

AI comments on the difficulty of the content instead of just handling it.

**Flag:**
- "This is a nuanced question."
- "The answer is more complicated than it might seem."
- "There's a lot to unpack here."
- "This deserves careful consideration."

**Fix:** Handle the complexity. Don't announce it.

---

## Category 2 — Reasoning Tells

Patterns in how AI handles claims, uncertainty, and argument.

### 2.1 False balance

AI hedges every strong claim with a concession that weakens it.

**Flag:**
- "While largely X, there are cases where Y."
- "In many ways, Field is..."
- "For the most part, X."
- "In a sense, X."
- "In some respects, X."

**Fix:** Make the claim. If there's a real exception worth naming, name it specifically.

---

### 2.2 Qualifier cluster at the end

AI adds vague qualifiers to the tail of claims so it can't be wrong.

**Flag:**
- "...particularly in today's environment."
- "...especially as the industry continues to evolve."
- "...in the current market landscape."
- "...as conditions continue to shift."

**Fix:** Cut the tail. The claim stands or it doesn't.

---

### 2.3 Soft hedge before a strong claim

AI softens claims that should stand on their own.

**Flag:**
- "In many ways, Field represents..."
- "It could be argued that..."
- "There's a case to be made that..."
- "It's fair to say that..."
- "One could argue that..."

**Fix:** Remove the hedge. State the claim.

---

### 2.4 Agency attributed to abstractions

AI gives agency to data, markets, or numbers instead of to people or actions.

**Flag:**
- "The data tells us..."
- "The market suggests..."
- "The numbers reveal..."
- "The research shows..."
- "The trends indicate..."

**Fix:** Name who the data tells, or state the implication directly. "Seventy-eight percent of RIAs report X." Not "the data reveals advisor pain."

---

### 2.5 The false anecdote

AI sets a hypothetical scene to manufacture empathy instead of describing the actual condition.

**Flag:**
- "Imagine you're an advisor sitting at your desk..."
- "Picture a firm principal looking at three different dashboards..."
- "Think about what it feels like to..."

**Fix:** Describe the actual condition. "The advisor pulls data from three systems before a client meeting." No staged scenes.

---

### 2.6 Perfect logical flow

Every paragraph flows seamlessly into the next. Reasoning is airtight and even throughout.

Real writing has detours. A thought appears, gets qualified, then lands differently than expected. Ideas interrupt themselves. Emphasis shifts. A short sentence stops the reader cold.

AI writing is smooth all the way through. That smoothness is the tell.

**Fix:** If everything flows too cleanly, find where a real writer would have stopped, reconsidered, or compressed. Make those moves visible.

## Category 3 — Sentence-Level Tells

Faster to spot, easier to fix.

### 3.1 Transition crutches

AI uses transitional phrases to signal movement between ideas instead of just moving.

**Flag:**
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "It bears mentioning that..."
- "It's important to note..."
- "It's important to understand..."
- "Importantly," / "Notably," / "Interestingly," / "Significantly,"
- "Moreover," / "Furthermore," / "Additionally,"
- "That said," / "With that in mind," / "To that end,"

**Fix:** Delete the transition. Connect ideas directly, or let them sit as separate sentences.

---

### 3.2 Gerund openers

AI begins paragraphs or sentences with a gerund construction instead of a subject.

**Flag:**
- "Building on this..."
- "Recognizing that..."
- "Bringing together..."
- "Leveraging the foundation..."
- "Understanding the challenge..."

**Fix:** Rewrite to start with the subject or the action.

---

### 3.3 The "not just X, but Y" escalation

AI adds a second, elevated claim to manufacture drama.

**Flag:**
- "Field is not just a data layer. It's a transformation."
- "This isn't just about integration. It's about intelligence."
- "Not just faster. Fundamentally different."

**Fix:** Make one clear claim. The escalation performs drama without adding content.

---

### 3.4 Adverb hedging

AI softens claims with adverbs that add no meaning.

**Flag:**
- "quite" / "rather" / "fairly" / "somewhat" / "relatively"
- "largely" / "mostly" / "primarily" / "essentially"
- "fundamentally" / "inherently" / "genuinely"
- "particularly" / "especially" used as vague emphasis

**Fix:** Cut the adverb. If the modification is real, make it specific: not "largely successful" but "succeeded in seven of nine pilots."

---

### 3.5 Compound emotional adjectives

AI pairs adjectives to perform depth it isn't earning through content.

**Flag:**
- "thoughtful and nuanced"
- "bold and confident"
- "clear and compelling"
- "practical and visionary"
- "simple yet powerful"

**Fix:** Pick one or cut both. If neither word adds content, the sentence doesn't need them.

---

### 3.6 No fragments

If every sentence is grammatically complete, it's probably AI. Real writing uses fragments deliberately for rhythm and emphasis.

Bill's writing: "The work speaks." "The period earns its place." "Not fragments. Workarounds."

**Fix:** Find the moments that deserve a fragment. Use them.

---

### 3.7 Perfect parallelism

AI makes every element in a list or series the same grammatical structure and similar length. Real writing varies shape and length — the variation is the rhythm.

**Fix:** Read the list aloud. If every element sounds the same length, break one of them.

---

### 3.8 Vocabulary variation to avoid repetition

AI avoids repeating words by instinct — it will cycle through "advisors," "practitioners," "wealth managers," and "financial professionals" across a paragraph.

Bill repeats key words deliberately for emphasis: "The picture is fragmented. The picture should be whole." Not "the picture is fragmented. That unified view should already exist."

**Fix:** If a word appears multiple times and it's the right word, keep it. Don't vary for the sake of varying.

---

## Category 4 — Opener/Closer Tells

AI has characteristic ways of starting and ending sections.

### 4.1 Epochal openers

AI frames content against a broad historical or market moment instead of starting with the specific condition.

**Flag:**
- "In today's rapidly evolving landscape..."
- "As wealth management continues to change..."
- "Given the current state of the industry..."
- "In an era defined by..."
- "Amid the growing complexity of..."

**Fix:** Start with the specific condition.

---

### 4.2 "At its core" openers

**Flag:** "At its core, Field is..." — AI windup before a definition.

**Fix:** State what it is. Drop the windup.

---

### 4.3 Aspirational closers

AI closes sections by invoking a future state or shared aspiration.

**Flag:**
- "...and that's the future we're building toward."
- "...because that's what this industry deserves."
- "...that's what drives everything we do."

**Fix:** End on the claim, the fact, or the action. Not the aspiration.

---

### 4.4 The emotional tag

AI adds an emotional bridge after the argument is already made.

**Flag:**
- "And that's what makes Field different — not just for firms, but for the advisors who run them."
- "That's not just good for the firm. It's good for the advisor."

**Fix:** The argument already carries the emotion. Remove the tag.

---

### 4.5 The "this is where X comes in" frame

**Flag:** "This is where Field comes in." — AI sets up a reveal instead of stating the thing.

**Fix:** State what Field does. Skip the frame.

---

## Enforcement Order

Run checks in this sequence:

1. **Read aloud.** Note anything smooth all the way through.
2. **Openers and closers** (Category 4) — fastest to spot.
3. **Structural patterns** (Category 1) — paragraph level.
4. **Reasoning patterns** (Category 2) — claim level.
5. **Sentence-level tells** (Category 3) — line level.
6. **Run banned-phrases.md.**

Flag every hit. Rewrite, don't patch. A sentence built on an AI tell usually needs to be rebuilt from the claim outward — not fixed in place.

If content passes all checks, run the read-aloud test again. AI writing that survives the checklist often still sounds smooth. Trust the ear.
