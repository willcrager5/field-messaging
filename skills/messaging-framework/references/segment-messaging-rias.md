## Segment Profiles

### $15B+ RIAs
Large independent RIAs managing $15B+ in AUM. Multi-office, multi-advisor firms with dedicated operations, compliance, and technology teams. Running a business at scale — acquisitions, recruiting, multiple custodial relationships, complex reporting, and a growing need to demonstrate firm value to investors.

### $1-15B RIAs
Independent RIAs managing $1-15B in AUM. Typically founder-led or small partnership. The founding advisor is still the center of gravity. They carry the client relationships, the institutional knowledge, and in most cases the operational burden of making the firm's systems work together.

## The Core Problem

### $15B+

The firm has grown, and every stage of growth added complexity. Each acquisition brought new systems. Each new office brought new workflows. Each custodial relationship brought new data feeds. The operations team scaled to manage complexity rather than to drive efficiency. The reconciliation burden didn't shrink as the firm got bigger. It multiplied.

The principals can't see the firm. Not in one place. Not in real time. Which advisors are growing? Which households are drifting? What does the book they acquired six months ago actually look like once it's been through the integration? The answers exist across five, eight, twelve systems — but they don't exist in a single view a principal can act on.

And the valuation question is always present. A firm whose data exists in fragments across a dozen platforms is harder to value than a firm whose business is legible in a single place.

This is not a problem the firm can solve by hiring more operations staff. Headcount manages the complexity; it doesn't resolve it. The problem is structural: there is no shared language underneath.

### $1-15B

The advisor chose each of these systems for a reason. Every one of them works. The problem is between them. Nobody built the connections, so the advisor became the connection. They pull client data from one system, cross-reference it in another, check the plan in a third, verify performance in a fourth. Every client review, every prospect conversation, every quarterly check requires the advisor to manually assemble a picture that should already exist.

The cost is personal. The person who carries the trust, the person the client chose, is spending their time reconciling data instead of advising clients. They find out about problems when the client calls. They miss the household that's quietly consolidating assets elsewhere. They can't see the practice whole because the picture is always assembled after the fact, from fragments.

This is not a technology problem the advisor can solve by buying another tool. Another tool is another language to translate. The problem is structural: there is no shared language underneath.

## Layer-to-Theme Mapping

### Translation (Data Layer) → Operating Leverage

**$15B+:**
The translation layer takes the infrastructure burden off the operations team. Every feed — from every custodian, every platform, every acquired firm's legacy systems — is translated into a shared language once, underneath. The ops team that was scaling to manage reconciliation complexity can focus on strategic initiatives instead.

**$1-15B:**
The advisor stops being the integration layer. Every feed from every system — custodian, CRM, planning, portfolio — gets translated into a shared language once, underneath. The hours spent pulling and reconciling data come back into the practice. At this scale, the advisor *is* the operations team. Operating leverage means the advisor's own time returns to the work that grows the firm.

---

### Comprehension (BI Layer) → Holistic Advice

**$15B+:**
When every system speaks the same language, the advisor sees the full client for the first time. Assets, plan, risk, household, outside holdings — across every custodian and every platform — in one coherent view. The advisor stops assembling fragments and starts giving advice that accounts for the whole picture. Every advisor across every office delivers the same depth of advice, regardless of which systems the client's data originally lived in.

**What to say ($15B+):**
> When the picture is whole, advice can be too. Every advisor across every office sees the full client — assets, plan, risk, household — in one view. The quality of the advice stops depending on how much time someone spent assembling the picture.

**$1-15B:**
The advisor chose this career to give advice, and spends most of their time doing something else. When the picture is whole, the advisor sees the full client — every account, every plan, every relationship — without pulling it together manually. The quality of the advice changes because the advisor is working from the whole picture, not from whatever they had time to assemble before the meeting.

**What to say ($1-15B):**
> When the picture is whole, advice can be too. You see the full client — assets, plan, risk, household — without assembling it yourself. The advice gets better because you're finally working from the complete picture.

**What not to say (both):**
- Don't say "360-degree view" or "holistic view." Both are industry clichés. Say what specifically becomes visible.
- Don't position this as a dashboard or reporting feature. This is about the quality of the advice the advisor gives, not a screen they look at.

---

### Comprehension (BI Layer) → Organic Growth

**$15B+:**
The principals can see the firm. Which advisors are producing. Which households are drifting. What an acquired book looks like once it's been integrated. How one office compares to another. Growth decisions run on signal instead of fragments, and M&A integration collapses from months to weeks.

At this scale, growth has two vectors: organic (wallet share, referrals, household deepening) and inorganic (acquisitions and advisor teams bringing books). Both depend on a coherent picture of the firm that lets principals act on signal rather than waiting for reports that are already stale.

**$1-15B:**
The advisor sees the practice whole for the first time. Which households are growing. Which clients haven't been reviewed in two quarters. Where assets are moving — consolidating toward the firm or drifting away. The signals that drive organic growth become visible because the picture is finally coherent.

Growth at this scale is organic, referral-driven, household-by-household. It comes from the advisor seeing what's in front of them and acting on it: the client who's ready for a deeper planning conversation, the household that's consolidating assets and could bring more, the relationship that hasn't been touched and is quietly at risk.

---

### Fluency (Modules) → A Firm Worth More

**$15B+:**
The firm acts on what it knows. A firm that understands itself — that can see its own book, measure its own performance across every dimension, demonstrate its value to any stakeholder — is worth more. Fluency is the layer where comprehension becomes capability.

Enterprise value at $15B+ is not abstract. These firms are valued, acquired, recapitalized, and merged. A firm whose data exists in fragments answers the valuation question slowly and incompletely. A firm built on Field answers it clearly, because the intelligence is in the system — visible, measurable, demonstrable to any outside party.

**$1-15B:**
The practice becomes transferable beyond the founder. Every founding advisor will eventually face the succession question: what is this practice worth, and can it run without me? A practice where the intelligence lives in the founder's head is worth whatever the founder's willingness to stay is worth. A practice where the intelligence lives in a system is worth more — measurable, demonstrable to a successor, a potential acquirer, or a next-generation partner.

## Lead Narratives

### $15B+

You built this firm by making the right decisions at every stage of growth. The right custodial relationships. The right technology for each part of the operation. The right acquisitions to expand the practice. Each decision worked. The problem is between them.

Every stage of growth added complexity. Every acquisition brought new systems and new data feeds. Every new office brought new workflows. Your operations team scaled to manage that complexity: reconciling data across platforms, assembling reports from fragments, making sure the numbers match before anyone can act on them. The team is good. The work shouldn't exist.

No platform connects the advice your advisors give to the business you run. The principal's view of the firm is assembled after the fact, from pieces that were never designed to fit together. Which advisors are growing? Which households are at risk? What does the book you acquired six months ago actually look like? The answers exist. They're scattered across a dozen systems that speak different languages.

Field goes underneath all of it. Every feed, every format, every legacy system, translated into a shared language once. Your platforms stay. Your relationships stay. What changes is that the picture becomes whole. The operations team works on strategic initiatives instead of reconciliation. The principals see the firm in real time. Growth decisions run on signal.

And over time, something else becomes clear. A firm that can see itself clearly is a firm that can demonstrate its value clearly. To an acquirer. To an investor. To the next generation of partners. Enterprise value compounds when the intelligence is in the system.

### $1-15B

You built this practice by choosing the right tools for each part of the job. The custodian holds the accounts. The CRM tracks the relationships. The planning tool runs the projections. The portfolio platform reports performance. Each one works. The problem is between them.

Nobody built the connections. So you became the connection. You spend hours pulling data from one system, cross-referencing it in another, assembling a picture that should already exist. The person your clients chose for advice is doing translation work instead.

Field goes underneath all of it. Every feed, every format, translated into a shared language once. Your tools stay. Your relationships stay. What changes is that the picture becomes whole. You see which households are growing. You see which clients need attention before they call. You see the practice. Not fragments of it.

And over time, something else changes. The practice becomes legible. Not just to you, but to a successor, a partner, a future acquirer. When the intelligence is in a system instead of in your head, the firm's value compounds.
